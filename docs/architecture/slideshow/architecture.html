Four rules that hold everywhere

When there's one right way, there's only one way

“How good is this pawn at this job?”

One place, from body and traits. A strong, axe-trained pawn fells the oak in half the swings. getWorkModifiers

“What bonuses apply right now?”

One tallier adds up gear, buildings and traits, with receipts. Your sharpened axe's +2 shows up here. ModifierSystem

“How do things move across the map?”

Heavy path-finding, in Rust, behind one door. The pawn routes around the pond to reach the oak. PathfinderService → WASM

“Can it run with no window open?”

Yes — the same game, driven by scripts. A test fells 1000 oaks headless to check the wood adds up. headless sim

Each has a “don't build a second one” rule burned in — which is why a new feature is usually new data, not a new code path.

PART II

The subsystems
and where they creak

Each specialist, up close: what it does, where it lives, and the one thing that makes you tilt your head. Every ◆ open question is drawn from the real code.

Pawns · the state machine

Each pawn is always in exactly one of 22 states

Idle, MovingToResource, Working, Hungry, Eating, Sleeping, Hauling, Socialising, Fighting, Fleeing, Hunting, Collapsed, Rescuing, BloodHunt, Crying, Hiding, Panicking… A lookup table picks the handler, so adding a state is one line.

Priority each tick: health & bleeding first (even for drafted pawns), then a combat interrupt, then needs, then work.

Where it lives

systems/PawnStateMachine.ts · handlers in systems/pawn/handlers/* · states enum in pawnStates.ts

◆ Why is it like this?The file's own header says the pawn AI “was decomposed out” as a 2026 hotspot — yet 1,851 lines remain. The easy 15 handlers left; the hard half (bleed, heal, death, collapse, aura radiation) never did. So “state-machine tick” is really state machine + a full medical simulation in one per-pawn loop.
specs: COMBAT-SYSTEM, SURVIVAL-HEALTH · code: systems/PawnStateMachine.ts (1851 lines)

Pawns · growth, aging & lineage

“Pawns grow” is a separate step — and that's the tell

Once per in-game day, each pawn banks a growth offer (four a year: three seasonal + a birthday that also ages them +1). An offer rolls all six stats; you later pick two to keep. It's opt-in meta-progression, not something a pawn does while acting — which is why it isn't an FSM state.

Where it lives

GameEngineImpl.processGrowth → pawnGrowthService.processDay · lineage in core/Lineages.ts

◆ Why is it like this? — your instinct was rightprocessDay isn't really “growth” — it's the catch-all “once a day” hook. In one loop it (a) banks stat offers, (b) ages the pawn, and (c) drives the entire lineage / awakening system. And it mutates pawns in place while step 11 threads immutable copies — a mutation-model mismatch in adjacent phases.
◆ And a second oneLineage keeps its own duplicate index of the trait catalog purely to dodge a circular import between Lineages and Culture — a layering problem papered over with two copies of the same data.
specs: LINEAGES I/II, TRAITS, RACE-SYSTEM · code: PawnGrowthService.ts, core/Lineages.ts

Pawns · health, wounds & death

A real body: nested parts, stacking wounds, three ways to die

Body parts nest and contain each other; destroying a vital one (a caved-in skull) is instant death, and severing cascades to everything inside. Wounds stack by damage type into one escalating entry; healing is deliberately slow, sped by sleep, food and tending.

Three death paths: lethal anatomy · bleeding out (blood ≤ 0) · a downed Collapsed state that can recover or be finished off.

Where it lives

core/BodyParts.ts · database/pawns/wounds.jsonc · applied in Combat.ts

◆ Why is it like this?The death rule lethalAnatomyCause is called from three places — inside combat's injury step, and independently by the per-tick pawn and mob reapers. “Dead means dead” is held together by three call sites staying in sync, not by a single choke point. Correctness-by-repetition.
spec: SURVIVAL-HEALTH · realism-first: bone destruction ≠ death, only a destroyed flesh-container kills

Combat

Swings, layered armour, and a block/parry axis

Weapons pull a damage profile; armour is layered — twelve slots map to body parts and mitigation walks outer-to-inner. Shields are a real second defence (block + parry + bash), distinct from dodge. A two-hander that also straps a shield is penalised, not forbidden.

Where it lives

systems/Combat.ts (2,746 lines) · ranged split into rangedCombat.ts to avoid an import cycle

◆ Why is it like this?Combat isn't pure damage resolution — _applyInjuryToEntity reaches in to credit lineage awakening progress mid-crush-hit, and the file also owns durability, natural weapons, bloodletting and the death rule. Combat “knows about” lineages and moods, so it can't be reasoned about alone.
◆ From the auditBecause block is CON + body-mass, every mob now negates ~1.8% of melee with no shield — unintended “bracing.” Your call whether to gate it behind actually holding a shield.
specs: COMBAT-SYSTEM, RANGED-COMBAT, CREATURE-COMBAT-OVERHAUL, EQUIPMENT-EXPANSION

Equipment & carrying

What you can wear, and how much you can lug

Carry budget is a strength-scaled slice of the pawn's own body weight; worn packs and carts add capacity, and even armour adds a little volume (“armour isn't a pure carry sink”). Volume, oddly, ignores strength entirely.

Where it lives

ItemService.getCarryBudget · slots in core/armorCoverage.ts · consumed by haul jobs

◆ Why is it like this?getCarryBudget(pawn, _state) takes a GameState it never uses (underscore-ignored) — and one caller fabricates an empty {} as GameState just to satisfy the signature. The function advertises a dependency that doesn't exist, forcing a caller to lie.
spec: EQUIPMENT-EXPANSION · code: services/ItemService.ts:688

Production · reserve-and-fetch

Items are always physical — a craft reserves, then fetches

A craft order starts as a wish. Each tick it tries to reserve all its inputs at once against a trial world — commit only if every input is free, else drop it so stock stays open for other orders. Once staged on the station tile, a pawn is sent to fetch, walk and work it. Furnaces are passive: no pawn, they cook on their own if lit.

Where it lives

jobService · services/jobs/craft.ts · passive in processPassiveProduction (ADR-016)

◆ Why is it like this?Whether a craft needs a pawn is decided in two independent places that must agree — the job generator skips passive stations, and the engine re-derives the same fact to know what to advance. Miss one and a furnace either produces nothing or spawns a phantom job no pawn can work.
specs: PHYSICAL-PRODUCTION, PRODUCTION-CHAIN I–IIII · code: services/jobs/craft.ts

Crafting · stations, tiers & recipes

~330 recipes across tiered stations

Recipes live at stations that ladder up in tier (craft_spot → makers_bench; hearth → oven; bloomery → finery). A lower tier stays usable after you build a higher one. Butchery stations give a yield bonus (more off a carcass); tool/smelting/cooking stations give speed instead.

Where it lives

recipeService · database/items/recipes.jsonc (132k) · station defs in buildings.jsonc

◆ From the audit“How is X made?” returns the first recipe producing it — so 25 recipes sharing an output were unreachable from the card (18 butchery ones fixed, 8 still shadowed). And the steel recipes are metallurgically inverted: the finery forge decarburises iron in reality, it never made steel; the blast furnace makes pig iron, not steel×3.

specs: PRODUCTION-CHAIN II/III/IIII, AUDIT (crafting) · steel & crystal reworks tracked, not yet built

Materials · the rulebook of stuff

Every material carries an identity — mostly

Materials are a fixed, hand-authored table keyed by item id (oak vs pine, granite vs marble). The leather rework split one-leather-per-animal into 17 per-animal leathers (buckskin, oxhide, bearhide, direwolf…), each with durability / warmth / beauty identity; five source wools too.

Where it lives

core/materialProperties.ts · material blocks in items.jsonc (410k)

◆ Why is it like this?“Procedural metals” isn't endgame-open — it's simply not modelled; materials are a static lookup, and the property cache assumes static-forever. Meanwhile a material's authored weight is never applied per-instance (no per-instance weight subsystem exists), and the old per-recipe materialBonuses turned out to be a display-only “cosmetic lie,” since removed.
specs: PROCEDURAL-METALS, PRODUCTION-CHAIN III, AUDIT (materials)

Jobs & work

Colony work is data, not a switch statement

Eleven job types (harvest, haul, fetch, construct, deconstruct, craft, refuel, repair, plant, caretake…) are declared as data, each bound to a generate/complete handler by id. Work category, labour priority, UI and claim-gating all flow from the definition. You can also right-click a pawn to force a job or need.

Where it lives

database/pawns/jobs.jsonc · JobService.handlers (ADR-017) · skill from getWorkModifiers (ADR-015)

◆ Worth knowingColony jobs (a `JobDef` each) and the FSM's internal kinds (eat, sleep, need) are two different registries — the FSM kinds have no `JobDef`. The old duplicated “job type → work category” switch was deleted; everything now routes through getJobWorkCategory. One place, on purpose.
specs: WORK-EXPERIENCE, DRAFTED-JOB-ORDERS · ADR-015 (single work model), ADR-017 (data-driven jobs)

The social layer

Relationships, mood and gossip — once a day

Pawns form culture-seeded relationships that drift through stages, hold conversations, fall in love, and feed a mood layer over their baseline. Big jumps arrive via hooks (a death, a rescue, a battle fought together). The heavy pass runs once per in-game day, in the events phase — zero per-tick cost.

Where it lives

SocialService.processSocialTurn · pure math in core/Social.ts · dialog split out

◆ Why is it like this?The daily pass is O(n²) over living pawns (~1,225 pairs at 50 pawns). There's no spatial partition — the whole design leans on a hard pawn-count ceiling, and the once-a-day gate is the mitigation. Dialog got its own file; mood/relationship upkeep stayed in the 1,215-line class — a half-finished decomposition.
spec: SOCIAL-LAYER · runs beside kingdoms in the daily events pass

Kingdoms, visitors & trade

A world beyond the colony — arriving on a clock

Other kingdoms drift in their own traits, make hidden-knowledge contact, and send visitors and caravans that you can barter with. Caravans need you to be at least a town. It all ticks once per in-game day, alongside the social pass.

Where it lives

KingdomService.processKingdomsDaily · pure half in core/Kingdom.ts · caravan parties in entity/kingdomParties.ts

◆ Why is it like this?A single arrival clock paces both visitors and caravans — a caravan is just a “trade chance” upgrade roll on a visitor. Trade is bolted onto the visit system rather than being its own economic loop, so caravan frequency is structurally chained to social-visit frequency.
specs: KINGDOMS-TRADE, VISITORS, RACE-SYSTEM (Phase 2)

Wildlife, spawning & husbandry

The map is alive — but you can't tame it yet

Creatures seed and spawn from lairs, run a per-tick brain (wander, hunt, hunger, flee), and leave corpses to butcher. That whole loop is live. Taming and animal husbandry are not.

Where it lives

EntityService facade over entity/entitySpawning · entityAI · entityLifecycle

◆ Why is it like this?The schema commits to a feature the sim doesn't have: a TamedAnimal type, a tamedAnimals save slot, and a tameable flag on every creature — with zero code reading any of it. Speculative data-modelling ahead of behaviour: it inflates the creature DB and every save with fields that currently do nothing.
specs: ENTITIES_SPAWNING, ANIMAL-HUSBANDRY (open) · husbandry = OPEN

Stealth

Being unseen is a filter on being seen

A pawn in a mob's line of sight must also lose a perception-vs-stealth roll to actually be noticed; darkness and weather shorten sight. The maths is pure and lives in one small file; the roll is cached per mob-pawn for ~2 seconds.

Where it lives

pure math in core/stealth.ts (ADR-032) · gated inside entityAI.isPawnDetected

◆ Why is it like this?The detection cache is stored on the mob and cleared when it gives up a hunt (“forget → re-stealth”). So stealth correctness is entangled with the hunt-FSM's give-up path — a bug in those state transitions silently breaks re-stealthing. The pure module owns the maths; the invariant lives in mob memory.
spec: STEALTH (open) · ADR-032

The living world · seasons & weather

Day, night, seasons, temperature, snow — one service

Light and tint cycle through the day; seasons bake a per-tile temperature that swings between noon and midnight; weather rolls and snow accumulates; fires project a heat field. All of it in one place.

Where it lives

services/EnvironmentService.ts — 1,682 lines, ~72 functions

◆ Why is it like this? — the clearest god-moduleOne file fuses five distinct domains: light, season, weather, a thermal/fire field, and gameplay crafting rules (fermentation & drying temperature windows). Those crafting windows sitting in the world-physics service is a plain misplacement — they leaked in because they happen to need temperature. The light-field and heat-field caches even copy-paste the same staleness logic instead of sharing one abstraction.
spec: SEASONS_WEATHER · biome variants, fog-of-war (deferred)

The map · grid & pathfinding

Movement maths is Rust, behind one TypeScript door

A* pathfinding and nearest-enemy queries are written in Rust, compiled to WASM, and reached only through an interface — callsites never touch the Rust directly. One body per tile; routes plan around occupied tiles. It even loads byte-identically under Node for headless replays.

Where it lives

PathfinderService → wasmPathfinderService → spatial-core (Rust, ADR-008) · occupancyService

◆ Why is it like this?WASM loads asynchronously, but the sim tick is synchronous. Nothing guarantees the pathfinder is ready before the sim starts — so every callsite carries a defensive “is it ready? else fall back to a JS scan / assume-connected” branch. The impedance mismatch is smeared across all callers instead of fixed once at boot.
spec: GRID-PATHFINDING · ADR-008 (spatial behind an interface)

Research

A tiered tree that unlocks buildings, items & tools

Research is tiered; each node lists prerequisites that must all be complete, and completing it unlocks buildings, items, abilities and a tool-tier bump. Progress accrues in the turn loop's research step.

Where it lives

services/ResearchService.ts · database/progression/research.jsonc

◆ Why is it like this?There's an empty systems/Research.ts — a one-line fossil of an abandoned systems→services migration that survives to mislead. And research applies its unlocks by mutating state in place (direct pushes) rather than the immutable-return style its neighbours use — an inconsistency in update discipline.
spec: RESEARCH-ENHANCEMENT (open) · three-tier design

Determinism & the headless sim

The same game, with no window — and byte-identical replays

A dev-only driver runs the same engine and the same 88-command registry with no worker and no screen — over HTTP (curl/agent), the DEBUG panel, and the test suite. Same seed + scenario + script ⇒ exactly the same world, every time.

Where it lives

game/headless/* · routes/api/sim/* (ADR-033) · guarded dev-only

◆ The contract that makes it workDeterminism is enforced: no Date.now() anywhere in the sim path — job, drop, craft and building ids are all turn-stamped (t42-…) — and module counters reset at session start. The whole AUDIT is driven through this: tick a box only when a headless run confirms it.
spec: HEADLESS-SIM · ADR-033 · this is the harness behind AUDIT.md

The open questions

Everything the map surfaced, on one board — the “why is it like this?” list worth deciding on purpose, not by drift.

The audit board

Decide these deliberately

◆ structureGrowth is the “once a day” grab-bag — stat offers + aging + lineage, mutated in place. Split the daily hook from stat growth?
